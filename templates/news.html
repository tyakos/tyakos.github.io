25/01/24: Projects, more projects and electronics

We have been hard at work on a lot of projects. The first involves drilling mud, second – large water pipes, third – liquid hyrdrocarbons, fourth involves clamp-on transducers. Exciting! But for now, I cannot share any further details.

But as we were working on those, we have really put our electronics through it's paces. Which showed us it's capabilities and limitations. And as it turned out, it is quite capabile indeed, so we are putting together licencing agreements for the interested parties.

In short, the low-power version of the board runs from 3.5-5V and is primarily intended for battery-powered applications. It can handle up transducers up to 1.5MHz with total insertion loss of up to -60db. The zero-flow drift is generally in the 25-150ps range for liquid-coupled applications, depending on the frequency and total inserion loss. The current version of the board supports multiplexing up to 16 transducers in pitch-catch and pulse-echo configurations. The data is transmitted via a serial connection (RS232/RS485 by default). Configuration is done using the same serial interface, with great flexibility. Powerful cross-correlation based algorithms are performed on the board with plenty of diagnostics data being generated. We are also working on a high-voltage version.

13/10/22: Testing and more testing!

We got our meter tested by the great team at WRc. Low flow performance is good with repeatability of 0.2-0.7% in the 50-100 l/h range. That indicates that our meters should have a turndown ratio of R400 with R800 as and option instead of the intially predicted R250. That's right, even more accurate measurements of minimal night flows!
High flow performance also looking fantastic with excellent repeatability. In fact, the meter performed well all the way up to 70 m3/h. A little bit more work is needed to achieve the next Q3 of 63 m3/h as performance at the corresponding Q4 (78.75 m3/h) is not yet stable, however preliminary data suggests that it is achievable.
Overall, we are quite pleased with the results and are hoping to fix all the remaining hiccups very quickly.

Secondly the continued internal tests have allowed us to significantly improve the temperature performance of the meters.
Together with the external test results this brings us very close to the end of the testing and I am very much looking forward to demonstrating the full potential of this technology.


20/08/22: Testing

We built a new flow rig that has allowed us to do a lot of controlled testing. So far we have established that the meter needs between 20D and 40D upsteam and about 10D downstream to achieve full accuracy, but perhaps the requirements can be relaxed to achieve the ±2% required in the OIML R49 class 2. Repeatability is looking great in the 0.2 - 8 m/s range, down to about 0.2-0.5%. In the coming weeks we have planned testing at an external facility to verify these results.

16/03/22: More water meter sizes, pressure ratings and lid demo

When calculating the minimum wall thickness for a plastic pipe a safety factor of around 3 is usually used at the operating pressure. We have done static pressure simulations to determine what is the maximum size of meter we can go up to at 16 bar.
And that is 100mm! In fact, our current 50mm prototype is about 2 times stronger than it needs to be. Which makes sense, our wall thickness is 4.5mm, the plastic is about two times stronger than regular pipe PVC, and PN16 PVC pipes are typically 3.7mm thick.
Moreover, larger meters will be more accurate as lower flows (in terms of m/s) and allow two ultrasonic paths to be used in order to maintain accuracy at high flows. So ultimately a higher turndown ratio can be achieved.
